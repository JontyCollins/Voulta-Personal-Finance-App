Why Wait to Invest? Every Day You Don't, You're Getting Poorer

By Jonty · 5 May 2026

There's a slow puncture happening to your money, and most people are sleeping through it. Literally. In 2025, UK savers lost almost £7 billion in purchasing power while they were asleep — roughly £122 per adult evaporating into the night, while their cash sat in accounts paying less than inflation.1 No alarm went off. No notification pinged. But the money was worth less when they woke up than when they closed their eyes.

That's not a one-off. It's not a bad year. It's the default setting of the modern economy: your cash is designed to lose value over time. The only question is whether you're going to do something about it or keep hitting snooze.

I'm going to make the case that waiting to invest is the most expensive mistake you can make — more expensive than picking the wrong fund, more expensive than a market dip, more expensive than almost anything else you could get wrong. And I'm going to show you why, with a story about three friends who made very different choices.

The slow, silent theft happening to your savings

Let's get the numbers out first, because feelings are nice but data changes behaviour. UK inflation hit 3.4% in December 2025, well above the Bank of England's 2% target.2 Meanwhile, the average interest rate on easy-access savings accounts sat at a pathetic 1.94%.3 If you can do basic maths, you already see the problem: your savings were losing about 1.5% of their real value every single year, just sitting there "safely" in the bank.

Over the full year, Fidelity International crunched the numbers and found that UK households collectively lost £17.6 billion in real purchasing power once inflation was accounted for.4 Not lost as in "the stock market dipped." Lost as in gone. Permanently. The money was still in the account, but what it could buy had shrunk.

Saving isn't safe anymore. Saving is a guaranteed slow loss, dressed up in the language of prudence.

And this isn't a short-term blip. Over a 20-year horizon, the Barclays Equity Gilt Study found that UK cash lost an average of -1.8% per year in real, inflation-adjusted terms. Equities, by contrast, returned +3.1% per year after inflation over the same period.5 That gap — nearly five percentage points, compounded over decades — is the difference between comfortably affording your life and quietly falling behind.

Cash has never been a worse bet

I want to pause on this because it's the single most important financial concept most Brits never learn: the risk of holding cash is not zero. It feels safe. It feels responsible. But the data says that over every 20-year period stretching back to 1988, UK stocks have consistently beaten inflation, while cash has failed to do so in a quarter of cases.6

Fidelity's analysis looked at every rolling 10-year period since the late 1980s. UK equities beat inflation 95% of the time. Cash managed it just 58% of the time.7 That means if you held cash for a decade, there was nearly a coin-flip chance you ended up poorer in real terms. If you held stocks for a decade, your odds of staying ahead were almost certain.

If you want a visceral example, IG ran the numbers on Cash ISAs versus the FTSE 100 going all the way back to 1999. Someone who maxed out their cash ISA allowance every year since ISAs launched would have seen just £23,199 in real wealth creation after inflation. Someone who put the same money into a FTSE 100 tracker? £157,591 in real, inflation-adjusted wealth.8 Same contributions. Same timeframe. A £134,392 difference. That's not a small gap — that's the cost of playing it "safe."

A tale of three friends: Maya, Dan, and Sam

This is where the abstract becomes real. Meet three people who all turned 21 in the same year. Each of them had £100 spare at the end of every month — not a huge sum, about the cost of a couple of takeaway dinners and a streaming subscription. Here's what they did with it.

Maya got curious. She'd read somewhere that the stock market had historically returned about 7% a year over the long run, and she had time on her side. So at 21, she set up a standing order: £100 a month into a broad global index tracker. She didn't overthink it. She didn't check the FTSE every morning. She just let it run. After ten years, at age 31, she stopped contributing entirely — life got expensive, she had other priorities — but she left everything invested. She never touched it again.

Dan got cautious. He also saved £100 a month, but he kept it in cash. He was waiting for the "right moment" to invest — that mythical point when markets are cheap, the news is calm, and everything feels safe. Occasionally he'd get close to pulling the trigger, but then the headlines would turn negative and he'd talk himself out of it. His money sat in a savings account earning whatever the bank felt like paying. He was convinced he was being prudent.

Sam got distracted. His twenties were a blur of rent, rounds, and recurring payments. Investing felt like something for later — people in suits, people with money, people who weren't him. At 31, something clicked. He decided to start investing £100 a month, exactly like Maya had done a decade earlier, and he kept at it for the next twenty years.

Now fast-forward to their 51st birthdays. Let's see where they stand.

Maya (the early starter)

Total contributed: £12,000 (£100/month for 10 years).
That money sat invested for 30 years in total — 10 years of contributions, then 20 years of pure compounding. At a 7% annual return, her pot grew to approximately £68,600.9

Maya contributed less than anyone else. She stopped contributing at 31. But time did the heavy lifting for her. That's what compounding does: it takes modest, consistent inputs and turns them into something that looks almost unfair.

Sam (the late starter)

Total contributed: £24,000 (£100/month for 20 years, starting at 31).
Sam put in twice as much as Maya — double the contributions, double the discipline. At the same 7% annual return, his pot reached approximately £52,600.

Let that sink in. Sam contributed £12,000 more than Maya but ended up with £16,000 less. He worked twice as hard for a smaller result. The only difference was the decade Maya had at the start — money she invested when she was 21 that Sam's money never got to catch up to.

Dan (the timer)

Total saved: £36,000 in cash over thirty years.
Dan never really got in. He kept waiting for the perfect moment, and the perfect moment never arrived. His cash earned roughly 2% in interest — but inflation eroded almost all of it. After 30 years, the real purchasing power of his savings had barely moved. Meanwhile, research from Schroders found that trying to time the market and missing just the 30 best days over a multi-decade period slashed annual returns from 11.4% to 7.0% — and that's if you were lucky enough to only miss 30 days.10 The Association of Investment Companies found that missing the 20 best days over 20 years cut your final investment value by 43%.11

Dan didn't lose his money in a crash. He lost it to inertia, disguised as caution. The so-called "safe" choice turned out to be the riskiest one of all.

The best day to start investing was ten years ago. The second-best day is today. Every day you wait, the maths gets a little bit worse.

This isn't a hypothetical. Research from JP Morgan Personal Investing found that someone who invested early in the tax year into a global tracker was nearly £17,000 better off after ten years than someone who waited until the last day.12 The pattern holds across every study, every market, every timeframe: time in the market beats timing the market. Always.

If you're young, you're holding a weapon you don't realise you have

There's a quiet revolution happening among young UK investors, and it's one of the most encouraging things I've seen in personal finance. According to Moneybox, 54% of 25-34 year olds chose to invest in the last year — a 13% increase since 2023.13 Among 18-25 year olds, 52% now invest. The appetite for long-term wealth building among younger generations has climbed from 18% in 2023 to 38% in 2025 among 25-34 year olds.14

This matters because youth is not just an advantage in investing — it is the advantage. Every year you're invested is a year your money is working while you're sleeping (and actually gaining value rather than losing it, unlike the £122-per-adult inflation tax mentioned earlier). You don't need a high salary. You don't need to pick the next Nvidia. You just need time, consistency, and a broad, cheap index fund.

A global tracker — something tracking the MSCI World or FTSE All-World — gives you a slice of the world's largest companies for near-zero effort. The Fidelity MSCI World Index fund returned over 18% in sterling terms in 2025 alone.15 Not every year will look like that. But over decades, the average is what matters, and the average has been remarkably consistent.

If you're 21 today and you put £100 a month into a global tracker at 7%, by 61 you're looking at roughly £262,000 — from a total contribution of just £48,000. If you wait until 31 to start, that same £100/month at 7% gives you about £122,000 by 61. Same monthly effort. Same total contributions from that point forward. The only thing that changed was when you started. And it cost you £140,000.

What to actually do about it

I'm not going to tell you to give up your daily coffee or cancel your Netflix. That's noise. Here's what actually matters:

  • Open a Stocks & Shares ISA. You can contribute up to £20,000 per year, and everything inside grows tax-free. No capital gains tax. No dividend tax. No brainer. If you don't have one, open one this week.
  • Pick a broad global index tracker. Something like the iShares MSCI World ETF or the L&G Global 100 Index Trust. Low fees (under 0.2%), massive diversification, no need to pick winners. You're betting on human ingenuity and global capitalism, not one company's quarterly earnings.
  • Automate it. Set up a direct debit for the day after payday. £50, £100, £200 — whatever you can afford. The amount matters less than the consistency. Treat it like a bill to your future self, same as the 50/30/20 approach I've written about before.
  • Ignore it. Check once a year if you must. The people who underperform are the ones who check daily, panic on red days, and try to get clever. Don't be Dan.

Fidelity estimated that if just a quarter of UK household cash savings — around £470 billion — had been invested instead of left in cash, savers could have gained £44 billion in real, post-inflation wealth in 2025 alone.16 Don't let your money be on the wrong side of that equation.

Stop waiting for the sign

I get why people wait. Markets feel volatile. The news is always scary. There's never a moment where everything looks calm and obvious. But waiting for the perfect entry point has been a losing strategy for over a century of market history, and it's not going to suddenly start working now.

Your cash is melting. Not dramatically — no smoke, no sirens — but steadily, year after year, while you wait for the "right time." The right time was yesterday. The next-best time is today.
